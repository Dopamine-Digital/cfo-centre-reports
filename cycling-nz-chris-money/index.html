Funding concentration and revenue mix

With NZ$5.25M/year from HPSNZ making up the majority of income and only ~5,000 paying members, CNZ is heavily exposed to any shift in government funding policy. Sport NZ has publicly flagged the question of "who should pay" for CNZ's core functions. Organisations this dependent on a single funder typically leave 2-4% of total revenue unoptimised because commercial, membership and event income streams are under-resourced.

Event and programme cost recovery

CNZ runs national championships, World Cup events and selection programmes across BMX, Road, Track, MTB and Para disciplines. Sport NZ's own case study noted CNZ derives "zero or negative financial return" from World Cup and major road tour involvement. Without per-event P&L tracking and cost recovery models, the subsidy from high-performance funding quietly erodes the budget available for development and participation.

Commercial revenue underweight

Despite Paris Olympic success (five track medals), CNZ's commercial and sponsorship income appears significantly below comparable NZ sport bodies. The loss of a major sponsor was flagged as a critical pressure point. Post-Olympics is the highest-value window for commercial deals, and without a structured sponsorship valuation and sales process, that window closes quickly. NZ sport bodies your size typically recover NZ$25K-40K/month in untapped commercial value.

NZ$2,500–4,000

Multi-discipline cost allocation

CNZ operates as an umbrella across BMX NZ, Road & Track, Mountain Bike NZ, Para Cycling and Schools. Each shares the Avantidrome facility, HP staff and administration. Without robust activity-based costing, overhead is typically spread evenly rather than allocated by actual usage. This obscures which programmes are financially sustainable and which are being cross-subsidised, making board-level resource decisions harder.

NZ$1,500–2,500
Est / mo

Cash flow timing vs HPSNZ funding cycles

Government funding arrives in set instalments, but athlete campaigns, international travel and event costs are lumpy and seasonal. With a Finance Manager rather than a strategic finance leader, cash flow forecasting and working capital management are likely reactive. Sport bodies in the NZ$7-10M band that tighten debtor management, negotiate supplier terms around funding cycles and maintain rolling 13-week cash flow models typically recover 1-2% of turnover in avoided overdraft costs and better procurement timing.
